01 · Learning map

A level is not a label.
It is a communicative range.

The mistake is thinking “A1 is bad” and “C2 is perfect.” A better view: each level controls a wider range of meaning. A1 survives. A2 manages routine. B1 explains. B2 argues. C1 nuances. C2 shapes meaning with style.

Input

What you can understand

Words, phrases, main ideas, details, implied meaning, tone, humor, and abstract argument.

+
Output

What you can produce

Answers, routines, stories, opinions, explanations, arguments, negotiations, and elegant expression.

Important: students do not “jump” levels by memorizing advanced words. They advance when they can use language under pressure: listening, responding, correcting themselves, expanding ideas, and staying clear.

06 · Class plan

How to teach this as a live session.

A strong class needs contrast, production, correction, and reflection. This route works for a 60–80 minute lesson.

10 min

Level awakening

Show the six levels. Ask students to self-place, then challenge them with one sentence from the ladder.

20 min

Level stations

Students choose one level below and one level above their comfort zone. They complete recognition and sentence-building tasks.

20 min

Same idea upgrade

Use the tapestry section. Students transform a simple A1 message into B1, B2, and C1 versions.

10 min

Exit performance

Each student speaks for 30 seconds using the target level. The teacher corrects clarity, range, and control.

Final principle

Do not study English as a pile of words.
Train it as a ladder of meaning.

At every level, ask: What can I understand? What can I say? How clearly can I connect ideas? How much pressure can I handle without freezing?
